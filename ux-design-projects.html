UX-Design Group Project

Case Study:

Title of project: Project Dreamcatcher

This collaborative UX project focused on designing a meaningful digital solution to empower Indigenous youth by fostering cultural connection, leadership, and belonging. Our team began by exploring a significant challenge identified through research: many Indigenous youth, particularly those raised outside of their traditional communities or living in urban environments, experience uncertainty about where they belong. These experiences can contribute to social stigma, discrimination, and a diminished sense of cultural identity and pride.

Using the Design Thinking process, we worked to better understand the needs of our users, challenge our initial assumptions, and reframe the problem. Through collaborative ideation and user-centered discussions, we developed the guiding question:

"How might we create leadership opportunities for Indigenous youth themselves so that they can define what culture and belonging looks like for their generation?"

This question became the foundation for our design decisions, inspiring the creation of a virtual platform that supports mentorship, accessible learning, and opportunities for Indigenous youth to build community, celebrate their culture, and develop as future leaders.

The Process

With our "How Might We" statement established, we defined a set of research goals to better understand the experiences, needs, and challenges of Indigenous youth. To gather meaningful insights, our team designed and distributed a user survey, receiving responses from 10 participants.

After collecting the data, we conducted an affinity mapping exercise to identify common themes, patterns, and recurring pain points. By organizing similar responses into meaningful groups, we uncovered key insights that informed our understanding of user needs. These findings guided the development of our research goals, which became the foundation for the design decisions made throughout the remainder of the project.

Personas

Key Insights

Using the insights gathered during our research phase, we designed a virtual platform centered on accessibility, connection, and leadership development for Indigenous youth. The platform allows users to register for live workshops where they can engage with peers, Elders, mentors, and community members in real time, fostering meaningful connections and shared learning experiences.

Recognizing that not all users can attend live sessions, we also included the option to access downloadable pre-recorded workshops, allowing participants to learn at their own pace and on their own schedule. To further support personal growth and community connection, we incorporated a mentorship feature that enables youth to connect with mentors for guidance, encouragement, and leadership development.

With these core features defined, we translated our ideas into low-fidelity wireframes. This allowed us to explore the platform's structure, user flows, and information architecture early in the design process, ensuring the experience aligned with both user needs and our project goals before moving into higher-fidelity designs.

Conclusion

This project demonstrated how thoughtful, research-driven UX design can help address meaningful community challenges. By listening to the experiences, the final solution evolved into a virtual conference platform that creates accessible opportunities for learning, mentorship, and cultural connection.

This project reinforced that designing for impact goes beyond creating functional interfaces; it requires understanding lived experiences, respecting cultural perspectives, and empowering users to shape their own narratives. The guiding question,"How might we create leadership opportunities for Indigenous youth themselves so that they can define what culture and belonging looks like for their generation?" remained at the center of every design decision.

As an aspiring UX designer, this experience strengthened my skills in user research, affinity mapping, accessibility, information architecture, wireframing, prototyping, and iterative design. More importantly, it highlighted the responsibility of designing inclusive digital experiences that amplify community voices rather than speaking for them. The result is a platform that supports connection, encourages cultural pride, and provides Indigenous youth with meaningful opportunities to learn, lead, and belong.

UX-Design - The Digital Commute Project

Case Study:

The Process:

I started with a problem statement of: "Full time work from home professionals struggle to disconnect from work at the end of the day due to the physical blurring of personal and professional spaces". Through utlization of the design thinking process, I came up with a how might we statement of "How might we assist work from home employees in establishing clear boundaries between their professional and personal lives within the same space?""

I brainstormed research goals that I thought would help get me closer to discovering ways to help remote professionals when separating "work life" from "personal life." I came up with survey questions and then conducted the survey, which had 6 respondants. I affinity mapped their responses and created persona's based on the information gathered.

After the responses were mapped and categorized into similar responses, I discovered some key insights, which are: people lack a physical, purposeful transition from work to personal time, which created anxiety, uncertainty and "blur" between work and personal time. Based on the research I also found that participants want something that indicates or gently reminds them that it is time to stop working.

The Concept:

Once I had a vision in mind and clear understanding of the pain points, I created low-fidelity wireframes to map out my ideas. Once I was satisfied, I turned those into high-fidelity wireframes.

  • Screen 1 - Gently nudges the user to stop working without causing anxiety.
  • Screen 2 - Clear the user's working memory so they don't carry the stress into their evening.
  • Screen 3 - Provides a highly tactile, satisfying physical action to close out the workday.
  • Screen 4 - Gives options for a physical or mental transition from “work” to “personal” time.
  • Screen 5 - Reinforces the boundary of work and personal time and rewards the user for completing the habit.

The End Result:

I developed the idea for an app called "The Digital Commute" The Digital Commute is an app that helps the user disconnect at the end of their work day. It allows the user to catalogue their achievements for the day and set goals for the next day. It also provides an opportunity for transition from "work time" to "personal time". After doing analyzing the data and coming up with key inisghts, I feel this app will help to alleviate some of the common problems when dsconnecting from "work time" to "personal time".
